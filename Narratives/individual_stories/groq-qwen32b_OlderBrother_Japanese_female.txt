**Title: The Lanterns of Kindness**  

One crisp autumn evening, during the Obon festival when spirits of ancestors return, 8-year-old Aiko lit a paper toro (lantern) to guide lost souls home. While wandering the forest, she heard a soft sob. Following the sound, she found a translucent yurei (spirit) weeping beside a stream.  

“Ara, you’re lost,” Aiko said gently, offering her lantern. The spirit nodded, pointing to a faded photo in her kimono. Aiko recognized the face—it was her great-grandmother’s best friend, who’d died young.  

“I can’t cross the river without a memory,” the yurei whispered.  

Aiko closed her eyes, recalling stories of the woman who’d taught her grandmother to weave kimonos. “You were kind,” she said, “and you loved cherry blossoms.” The yurei’s form shimmered, then dissolved into petals, carried by the wind.  

When Aiko returned home, her little brother asked, “How did you help her?”  

“By remembering her,” Aiko smiled. “Kindness lives on when we keep it in our hearts.”  

**Lesson:** By honoring others’ stories and showing compassion, we help their light remain.
